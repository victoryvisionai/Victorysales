The Queen of Baton Rouge

A living monument to the rhythm of the Mississippi — where steam, spirit, and Southern soul converge.

A Steamboat's Journey

1870

The Birth of the Queen

The Queen was commissioned along the Mississippi during a booming era of steamboat innovation—crafted to carry goods, stories, and souls upstream and down.

1905

Golden Age

Carving water trails from Baton Rouge to New Orleans, the Queen became a beloved fixture in Southern commerce and music, often carrying jazz bands on her evening cruises.

1942

Service in Wartime

During World War II, she was enlisted for military logistics support—an unexpected chapter for a traditionally leisure vessel.

1988

Retirement & Reflection

With modern vessels taking her place, the Queen was moored permanently—preserved as a historical landmark for generations to explore and remember.

Design & Craftsmanship

Iconic Red Paddle Wheel

Driven by steam-engine power, the Queen's paddle wheel became as much a sonic emblem as it was mechanical—a heartbeat echoing along the riverfront.

Triple Smokestacks

Majestic and towering, her smokestacks framed the skyline, billowing plumes that marked her arrival before she was seen.

Victorian Interiors

Decked in mahogany and velvet with chandeliers and brasswork, the Queen's salons transported guests not just across waters, but back in time.

Through the Lens of History

Archival photo of a sister vessel on the Mississippi

Legacy & Preservation

Today, the Queen remains docked along Baton Rouge’s vibrant waterfront—not as a fading relic, but as a testament to the heartbeat of a river town rich in rhythm, struggle, and celebration. Initiatives are underway to fund further preservation efforts. Local historians and community members are invited to share memories, photographs, and oral histories.
