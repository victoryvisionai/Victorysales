The Queen of Baton Rouge

A Steamboat's Journey

1870

The Birth of the Queen

The Queen was commissioned during a golden age of river trade. Built with Southern craftsmanship, she was designed to transport goods and passengers while embodying the elegance of Victorian design.

1905

Golden Age Voyages

The Queen became a cultural phenomenon—hosting jazz musicians, Mardi Gras parties, and twilight cruises between Baton Rouge and New Orleans. Her decks echoed the rise of Dixieland sounds and festive Southern nights.

1942

Service in Wartime

Adapted for military use during WWII, she ferried supplies and troops along the Mississippi. Crews painted her hull in camouflage, and her paddle wheel churned toward service instead of celebration.

1988

Retirement & Reflection

Moored ceremoniously at the Baton Rouge riverfront, the Queen now stands preserved—welcoming sightseers, school groups, and proud locals to admire her enduring grace and learn from her voyage.

Design & Craftsmanship

Red Paddle Wheel

The Queen’s rear-mounted, steam-powered paddle wheel is an icon against the horizon—its rhythmic churn once harmonizing with steam whistles and river songs.

Triple Smokestacks

Three imposing smokestacks mark the sky as sentinels of tradition—power and grace embodied in wrought-iron adornments and soft plumes of smoke.

Opulent Interiors

Rich mahogany paneling, velvet settees, and crystal chandeliers crafted the interior elegance of the Queen—once hosting dances, dinners, and moonlit proposals.

“Step aboard the Queen, and you step into a dream carved in steam and wood.”

Legacy & Preservation

Today, the Queen rests serenely along the bustling Baton Rouge waterfront. She is more than a museum piece—she is a living memory. Community-led efforts are preserving her structure and stories. From oral histories to guided tours, each initiative invites you to be part of keeping her heart beating through remembrance and reverence.
